50 OET Medical Words Candidates Always Misspell

The Writing sub-test penalises every spelling error. These are the words that trip up even experienced healthcare professionals — with correct British spellings and the mistakes to avoid.

April 2026 8 min read OET Writing 50 words covered

In the OET Writing sub-test, every spelling error counts against you. The assessors are looking for the kind of accurate clinical documentation expected from a registered healthcare professional — and consistent misspellings of medical terminology signal a lack of proficiency that lowers your grade.

The problem is that many of these words follow British English conventions that differ from American English, or contain Greek and Latin roots with counter-intuitive letter combinations. If you trained in a country where American spelling is standard, you are at particular risk.

Key fact: The OET uses Australian and British English spelling conventions. Words like diarrhoea (not diarrhea), haemoglobin (not hemoglobin), and oesophagus (not esophagus) must be spelled the British/Australian way to be marked correct.

Why spelling matters more in OET than you think

The OET Writing sub-test asks you to write a referral letter, discharge summary or other clinical document based on case notes. Assessors mark your response on five criteria — but linguistic appropriateness (which includes spelling) accounts for a significant portion of the grade.

A candidate who misspells drug names, anatomical terms, or procedure names is demonstrating exactly the kind of carelessness that would concern a clinical supervisor. Even one or two persistent errors can drop you from a B to a C on that criterion.

Drug names are especially risky. Misspelling a medication name in a real clinical document could cause patient harm — assessors know this, and they mark drug spelling errors accordingly.

British vs American spelling — the OET trap

This is the most common source of errors for nurses and doctors trained in countries using American English. The OET follows British/Australian conventions throughout. The differences are systematic — once you learn the patterns, they become second nature.

The pattern: British English uses -ae- where American uses -e- (haemoglobin/hemoglobin), -oe- where American uses -e- (oedema/edema), and -isation where American uses -ization. Learn these three rules and you immediately eliminate a large category of errors.

4 patterns that explain most OET spelling errors

1. The hae-/ae- pattern

Words derived from the Greek haima (blood) use hae- in British English. Words derived from aima use ae-. American English drops the a in both. Always add it back for OET.

  • haemoglobin, haemorrhage, haematology, haematoma
  • anaemia, leukaemia, septicaemia

2. The -isation vs -ization pattern

British English consistently uses -isation for procedure and process nouns. American uses -ization. In OET, always use -isation.

  • catheterisation, hospitalisation, cauterisation, embolisation, mobilisation, immunisation

3. Silent letter openings from Greek

Many medical terms begin with consonant clusters that contain silent letters. You must write the full cluster even though you don't hear it.

  • ps-: psychiatrist, psychosis, psychological
  • ph-: pharynx, photophobia, phalanx
  • ch- (as /k/): cholesterol, chemotherapy, chiropractor
  • pn-: pneumonia, pneumothorax

4. The -ectomy / -ostomy distinction

Two common surgical suffix patterns that candidates confuse:

  • -ectomy = surgical removal (cholecystectomy, nephrectomy, appendicectomy)
  • -ostomy = surgical opening (colostomy, tracheostomy, ileostomy)

Knowing the suffix meaning prevents the wrong one from appearing in your letter.

How to study OET spelling effectively

Passive reading of word lists does not build spelling ability. The research on spelling acquisition is consistent: you need active retrieval practice — hearing the word, recalling the spelling, writing it out, and getting immediate feedback.

  1. Hear, then spell — don't read the word and copy it. Cover the correct form, hear it spoken, write it from memory, then check.
  2. Focus on errors — the words you get wrong are the only ones worth repeating. Spending time on words you already know is inefficient.
  3. Short daily sessions beat long weekly sessions — 10 minutes every day will outperform 70 minutes once a week because of how memory consolidation works.
  4. British pronunciation helps — words like haematology and oesophagus are more transparent when spoken with a British accent because the vowel sounds match the spelling more closely than in Australian or American variants.
  5. Group by pattern, not by alphabet — practise all the -isation words together, then all the hae- words. Pattern recognition reduces the total number of things to memorise.

Exam tip: In the OET Writing sub-test you have time to review your letter before submitting. Build a habit of doing a final pass specifically checking the spelling of any medical term you are not 100% certain about. Flag words you know you sometimes get wrong and check those first.

A note on drug name spelling

Generic drug names follow International Nonproprietary Name (INN) conventions which are slightly different from both British and American English. The safest approach is to memorise the exact INN spelling of common drugs rather than trying to derive it from pronunciation rules.

Key drugs that frequently appear in OET case notes and are commonly misspelled:

  • metformin (not metphormin)
  • atorvastatin (not atorvostatin)
  • amlodipine (not amlodipin)
  • omeprazole (not omeprazol)
  • salbutamol (not salbutanol) — note British form; American form is albuterol
  • paracetamol (not paracetamol) — British; American form is acetaminophen
  • hydrochlorothiazide — spell it out in full, every time
